# Short-drama Adaptation Method

## Preserve identity, not every sentence

An adaptation should preserve the source’s causal spine, emotional promise, character logic, and distinctive motifs. It may compress chronology, merge minor roles, externalize internal thought, or move reveals when required by duration—but every change must be deliberate.

## Three ledgers

Maintain these mentally or in artifact metadata:

1. Canon ledger: explicit source facts and stable references.
2. Change ledger: each merge, omission, reorder, or invention and why it improves production.
3. Promise ledger: what the audience must understand or feel by each episode ending.

## Compression tests

Before removing or merging a beat, ask:

- Does a later event depend on it?
- Does it establish motive, relationship, rule, or object state?
- Is it the only evidence for a reveal?
- Can its function move into visible action or subtext?
- Would a source-aware viewer feel that the story’s identity was lost?

## Short-form episode design

- Hook: an immediate contradiction, threat, desire, reveal, or unusual image.
- Orientation: only the context needed to understand the current conflict.
- Escalation: at least one irreversible change.
- Turn: new information or action changes the scene’s direction.
- Exit: payoff, dilemma, or cliffhanger with a concrete next question.

Do not use arbitrary cliffhangers disconnected from character choice.

## Internal narration

Convert internal prose using the least expensive truthful method:

- visible behavior or object interaction;
- dialogue with conflict/subtext;
- reaction and juxtaposition;
- brief voice-over only when the internal fact cannot be dramatized cleanly.

Avoid narrating what the image already shows.
